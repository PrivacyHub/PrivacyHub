Fitness Tracker Privacy: Where Does Your Health Data Go?

November 20, 2025 5 min read By Asayel Hasan

What Data Do Fitness Trackers Collect?

Fitness trackers collect steps, heart rate, sleep patterns, GPS routes, calories burned, and workout sessions. Some models also track stress levels, oxygen saturation, and payment details. These devices continuously monitor your body's metrics, creating a comprehensive digital profile of your physical health, activity levels, and daily routines.

How Do They Track Habits?

Sensors continuously monitor your activity and sync data with mobile apps. They analyze long-term health patterns, daily routines, and physical performance. Advanced algorithms can detect changes in your health, predict menstrual cycles, identify stress patterns, and even detect potential health issues like atrial fibrillation. This data is often stored in cloud servers and may be used for research or product improvement.

What Are the Risks?

Location and health data can reveal daily routines, workplace locations, home addresses, and sensitive medical information. Third-party apps may access your data if permissions are granted, sometimes without your full understanding. Hackers could potentially misuse personal health information for blackmail, stalking, or identity theft. Insurance companies have shown interest in fitness data for underwriting decisions. In some cases, fitness tracker data has been used in legal proceedings without consent.

Types of Data Collected:

  • Steps and Activity: Daily step counts, distance traveled, active minutes
  • Heart Rate: Resting heart rate, workout peaks, heart rate variability
  • Sleep Patterns: Sleep duration, quality, wake times, REM cycles
  • GPS Routes: Running/walking paths, frequently visited locations
  • Calories Burned: Energy expenditure throughout the day
  • Workout Data: Exercise types, duration, intensity, performance
  • Biometrics: Stress levels, blood oxygen, skin temperature
  • Payment Information: If using contactless payment features

How to Protect Yourself

  1. Turn off public sharing features: Disable any options that share your activity publicly or with friends. Review privacy settings in the app.
  2. Carefully review app permissions: Check what data third-party apps can access and revoke permissions for apps you don't trust or use.
  3. Use strong passwords and 2FA: Secure your fitness tracker account with a unique password and two-factor authentication.
  4. Regularly update firmware and apps: Keep your device and associated apps updated for security patches.
  5. Disable location tracking when not needed: Turn off GPS for workouts that don't require route tracking.
  6. Review data retention policies: Check how long your data is stored and delete old data regularly.
  7. Opt out of research: Many fitness apps share anonymized data for research. You can usually opt out.
  8. Be careful with social features: Avoid connecting to social media or sharing achievements publicly.
  9. Check insurance programs: If your insurer offers discounts for sharing fitness data, understand exactly what they access.

Data Sharing with Employers

Some corporate wellness programs encourage or require fitness tracker use for insurance discounts. Be aware that this often means your employer or insurance company receives aggregated health data. Understand exactly what data is shared and whether it's truly anonymous before participating.

Deleting Your Data

When you stop using a fitness tracker, ensure you delete all cloud data before disposing of the device. Perform a factory reset and remove the device from your account. Request data deletion from the manufacturer if possible, as some data may persist on their servers even after you stop using the device.
