Smart TV Privacy Risks: What Data Your TV Collects and How to Stay Safe

December 10, 2025 7 min read By Asayel Hasan

What Data Do Smart TVs Collect?

Smart TVs collect much more than people realize. They track your viewing history, search terms, app usage, IP address, location data, and device ID. Many smart TVs use a technology called Automatic Content Recognition (ACR). ACR tracks what you are watching, even if it comes from a cable box, gaming console, or streaming device. If your TV includes a microphone or camera, it may also collect voice recordings and video data when voice features are enabled.

How Do They Track Viewing Habits?

ACR works by scanning small visual or audio signals from the content playing on your screen. This data is sent to the manufacturer's servers. Companies analyze this information to understand your interests and habits. It is often used to show personalized advertisements.

What Are the Privacy Risks?

Your viewing habits can create a detailed profile about your lifestyle, interests, and even beliefs. This data may be shared with advertisers and third-party partners. If security systems are weak, hackers may access your personal information.

Types of Data Collected:

  • Viewing History: Everything you watch, including time and duration
  • Search Terms: What you search for on your TV
  • App Usage: Which streaming apps you use and how often
  • IP Address: Your general location and internet provider
  • Device ID: Unique identifier for your specific TV
  • Voice Recordings: If voice control is enabled

How to Protect Your Privacy

  1. Turn off ACR: Go to your TV settings and disable Automatic Content Recognition or "Smart TV experience" features.
  2. Disable voice control: If you don't use voice features, turn off the microphone in settings.
  3. Cover or disconnect cameras: If your TV has a built-in camera, cover it when not in use.
  4. Update software regularly: Keep your TV's firmware updated to patch security vulnerabilities.
  5. Secure your Wi-Fi: Use a strong password and WPA3 encryption if available.
  6. Review privacy settings: Check your TV's privacy menu monthly and adjust data sharing preferences.

Additional Steps for Maximum Privacy

Consider using an external streaming device (like Apple TV or Roku) instead of your smart TV's built-in apps. These devices often have better privacy controls. You can also disconnect your TV from the internet entirely and use it as a "dumb" monitor with external devices.
