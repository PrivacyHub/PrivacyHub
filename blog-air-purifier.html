Smart Air Purifiers: Hidden Data Collection?

November 18, 2025 4 min read By Asayel Hasan

What Data Do They Collect?

Smart air purifiers monitor air quality, usage patterns, fan speed, location data, and app interactions. Some collect daily runtime and filter usage information. These devices track indoor air quality metrics including particulate matter, volatile organic compounds, humidity, and temperature. This data helps optimize performance but also creates a detailed picture of your indoor environment and habits.

How Do They Track Habits?

They record when the device is turned on or off and how often filters are changed. This data helps optimize performance and can be stored in the cloud. Advanced models learn when air quality typically deteriorates in your home (like during cooking or cleaning) and adjust automatically. This creates a profile of your daily activities and their impact on your indoor environment.

What Are the Risks?

Usage data may reveal daily routines, like when you are home or away, when you cook, clean, or sleep. Data may be shared with third-party service providers or app developers for product improvement or marketing. Hackers could potentially access device controls to disrupt operation or gather occupancy data. Air quality data combined with timestamps could reveal when you're home and what activities you're engaged in.

Types of Data Collected:

  • Air Quality Readings: PM2.5, VOC levels, humidity, temperature
  • Usage Patterns: When the device runs and at what speed
  • Fan Speed: Manual and automatic speed settings
  • Location Data: Your address for local air quality comparisons
  • Filter Status: When filters are changed, remaining filter life
  • App Interactions: How you control the device remotely
  • Runtime Data: Total hours of operation, daily averages
  • Wi-Fi Information: Network connection details

How to Protect Privacy

  1. Disable unnecessary app permissions: Review what data the app can access on your phone (location, contacts) and revoke permissions that aren't essential.
  2. Review privacy settings: In the device app, disable any options that share usage data with third parties or for research.
  3. Keep Wi-Fi secure: Use WPA3 encryption and a strong password for your network.
  4. Avoid public networks: Never connect smart devices to public or unsecured Wi-Fi.
  5. Update firmware: Regular updates patch security vulnerabilities. Enable automatic updates if available.
  6. Consider local control: Look for devices that can operate without cloud connectivity if you're concerned about data sharing.
  7. Check manufacturer's data policy: Understand what data is collected and how it's used before purchasing.
  8. Use a separate IoT network: Isolate smart devices on a guest network to limit access if compromised.

The Value of Air Quality Data

While air quality monitoring provides valuable health insights, consider whether you need cloud connectivity for this feature. Some purifiers can display air quality locally without sending data to the cloud. If you're comfortable with data collection, ensure you understand exactly what's being collected and how it's used.

Location Data Concerns

Many air purifiers ask for your location to provide local outdoor air quality comparisons. This links your device to your home address. If you're uncomfortable with this, consider using a nearby city or disabling this feature if the device still functions properly without it.
